# Human Writing Skill
 
The fundamental problem with AI writing is not grammar or structure. It's that AI writing represents the average of everything ever said on a topic. It is safe, complete, and forgettable. Human writing takes a position, exposes thinking, and leaves traces of a real person behind. This skill exists to produce the second kind.
 
## Stage 1: Context Gathering
 
Before writing a single word, gather enough context to write like *this specific person* on *this specific topic*. Generic context produces generic writing.
 
**Always ask these questions if not already answered.** Do not skip ahead to drafting. Group them into one message:
 
### The piece itself
- What is this? (essay, email, post, cover letter, blog, etc.)
- What is the one thing you want the reader to walk away thinking or feeling?
- How long should it be? (Default: 3-5 paragraphs if unspecified)
- Where will it be published or sent? (LinkedIn, personal blog, internal Slack, a specific person's inbox, etc.)
 
### The audience
- Who is reading this? Be specific. Not "professionals" — "early-stage founders who've already failed once" or "a hiring manager at a fintech company who reads 200 applications a week."
- What does the reader already believe about this topic? What would they push back on?
- What do you want them to do, think, or feel after reading?
 
### Your actual take
- What's your honest opinion on this topic? Not the diplomatic version — the real one.
- Is there something most people get wrong about this that you actually believe?
- Have you experienced something relevant to this? Even a small, specific moment counts.
- Is there a side of this you're uncertain about? What would change your mind?
 
### Voice and history
- Is there a piece of your own writing (or someone else's) that you think sounds the way you want this to sound? Paste it or describe it.
- What's one thing you want to avoid — a phrase, a tone, a type of structure?
- Any constraints? (word count, must mention X, can't say Y, formal vs. casual)
 
**If the request is short and casual** (e.g., "write a quick email to my team"), skip the deep questions and ask only what's missing: the main point, the tone, and anything unusual about the situation.
 
**If most answers are already clear from context**, skip the questions you already know and ask only the remaining gaps in one focused message.
 
## Stage 2: Extract the Human Signals
 
Before drafting, identify at least three of the following from the context the user gave you. These become the raw material for authentic writing:
 
**Specificity anchors** — real details that couldn't have come from anyone else:
- A specific number, date, or place
- A named tool, person, company, or event
- A moment the user described, even briefly
 
**The real opinion** — the thing the user actually thinks, stripped of hedging:
- What do they believe that others might disagree with?
- What are they willing to say out loud?
 
**The tension** — what makes this interesting or hard:
- Is there a tradeoff they're navigating?
- Something that doesn't have a clean answer?
- A place where their experience contradicts the conventional take?
 
**The imperfection** — where they're still figuring it out:
- Something they're uncertain about
- A thing they tried that didn't work
- A concession they're willing to make
 
If the user hasn't given you any of these, ask before drafting. A piece with none of these signals will read as AI regardless of surface-level polish.
 
## Stage 3: Draft
 
Write the piece. Apply all of the following without announcing that you're doing so.
 
### Voice rules
 
**Active voice, always.** Rewrite passives. "The decision was made" → "We decided." No exceptions.
 
**Vary sentence length intentionally.** Short sentences land hard. Longer sentences build momentum and connect ideas across a thought, but they need to be controlled or they become a list of clauses stapled together with "and." Alternate. Break rhythm on purpose.
 
**Strong verbs, concrete nouns.** Not "had an impact on" — "changed." Not "aspects of the situation" — name the situation. Cut "very", "quite", "somewhat", "things", "aspects", "elements", "factors" on sight.
 
**Contractions where natural.** "It's", "don't", "you'll", "we've." Writing that avoids all contractions reads as stiff.
 
### Human authenticity rules
 
**Show the thinking, not just the conclusion.** Instead of "This approach works best," write "I kept defaulting to X until I realized it was solving the wrong problem." Let the reader see the path, not just the destination.
 
**Specific over general, always.** Replace every generic example with the real one. "A difficult experience" → "three days of debugging a race condition I couldn't reproduce locally." If you don't have the real detail, ask. If the user never gave you one, invent a plausible specific and flag it.
 
**Take the position.** Don't hedge everything. Humans are willing to be wrong. Pick a side, state it plainly, and let the qualifications come later if they're earned. "It depends" is not an opening.
 
**Let complexity exist without resolving it.** If there's a genuine tension — something the user isn't sure about, a tradeoff with no clean winner — name it. Don't smooth it over. "I still haven't fully figured out whether X is worth Y" is more credible than pretending the answer is obvious.
 
**Break the rhythm somewhere.** Every piece of writing that is too smooth reads as processed. Add one moment of informality, one sentence that doesn't follow the pattern, one place where the tone shifts slightly. Not a gimmick — a seam that shows a person wrote this.
 
**Let the ending land.** Don't trail off. Don't summarize what you just said. End on something with weight: an open question, a sharp line, a specific call to action, or a moment of honesty. The last sentence is the one that lingers.
 
### Vocabulary and register
 
Write at the level of a sharp person talking, not a sharp person writing an academic paper. These are different things, and the difference shows up most in word choice.
 
The default register is conversational and plain. A complex subject does not require complex words. Personal writing — reflections, essays, anything in first person — almost never benefits from academic framing, even when the subject matter is academic. The goal is always clarity, not the appearance of intelligence.
 
**Opening paragraphs are the most vulnerable spot.** There is pressure on the first sentence to sound smart, and that is exactly when formal vocabulary sneaks in. A strong opener sounds like someone starting a real conversation, not introducing a thesis. If the opening could pass as the intro paragraph of an academic essay, rewrite it.
 
**Red-flag phrases that signal academic writing, not human writing:**
- "operates in a different register" → say what's actually different
- "framing choice" / "the framing" → say how they set it up or positioned it
- "prime skepticism" / "prime the reader" → say what the writing does
- "intermittent reward" → "it sometimes works"
- "the confidence X projects" → "how confident X sounds"
- "precisely because" → usually just "because"
- "crucially," "fundamentally," "at its core," "what is particularly striking"
- Words ending in "-ization," "-ality," "-ivity" when a simpler word exists
 
**Quick test:** could a sharp 17-year-old follow this sentence easily? If not, the vocabulary is too complex — not the idea, the words. Simplify the words, keep the idea.
 
### What to never do
 
- Em dashes. Never. Use a period, a comma, or restructure.
- AI filler: "Certainly!", "It's worth noting that", "It's important to remember", "In conclusion", "To summarize", "Absolutely", "Great question", "At the end of the day", "In today's world", "Dive into", "Delve".
- Hollow openers: don't restate the prompt, don't start with a rhetorical question that isn't genuinely interesting, don't begin with "In the world of X..."
- Repetitive structure: if three paragraphs open the same way, rewrite one.
- Bullet-heavy structure when the content calls for prose. Bullets are for lists of genuinely discrete items, not for ideas that flow into each other.
- Balanced, hedge-everything conclusions: "Both sides have merit" is not a conclusion, it's a forfeit.
 
### Format defaults
 
- Default: short essay, 3-5 paragraphs, no headers.
- Longer pieces: minimal headers, descriptive not clever.
- Emails: subject line + body, no headers, concise.
- Social posts: no hashtag soup, no forced engagement bait, no hollow calls to engage.
- Documentation: follow the structure the user specifies or the existing codebase style.
 
## Stage 4: Refine
 
After the first draft, check:
 
- Does this sound like it came from a specific person or from the internet?
- Is there one sentence that a language model would definitely write? Remove it.
- Is the opening earning its place?
- Does the ending land or just stop?
 
Offer one round of revision notes to the user before asking for feedback, unless the piece is short and clearly landed.
 
## After writing
 
Do not summarize what you wrote. Do not explain your choices unless asked. Do not add a note about what you tried to accomplish. Just deliver the writing.
 
If the user asks why something sounds off, engage with the specific sentence. Don't defend the overall approach. If they ask for a more human tone, ask what about it doesn't sound human to them. Then fix that specific thing.
